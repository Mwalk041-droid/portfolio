IDS 493 Capstone

Reflective Essay

Describing how my artifacts demonstrate my top three skills and how my academic experiences contributed to career readiness.

Author Michael Walker
Course IDS 493 — Interdisciplinary Studies Capstone
Institution Old Dominion University
Year 2026

Introduction

Cybersecurity is not a single-discipline field. Defending systems, responding to threats, and managing risk requires technical knowledge drawn from computer science and information systems, combined with the analytical thinking developed in risk management, philosophy, and communication courses. Over the course of my Bachelor of Science in Cybersecurity at Old Dominion University, I built a skill set that spans these disciplines. It reflects not just what I learned in core technical courses, but how interdisciplinary thinking made that knowledge usable in the real world.

The three skills I highlight in this portfolio are web application security and vulnerability remediation, cloud computing with AWS, and security operations. Each emerged from a combination of technical coursework, hands-on lab work, and real-world internship experience. Together, they represent the most marketable and career-ready skills I am bringing into the workforce.

Web Application Security and Vulnerability Remediation

My strongest area of applied skill is web application security. This skill grew directly out of my work as a Cybersecurity Analyst Intern at Earth Viability Center, where I identified and remediated live vulnerabilities including SQL injection, cross-site scripting (XSS), and cross-site request forgery (CSRF). These are three of the most common and damaging vulnerabilities listed in the OWASP Top 10 . I also designed and implemented a Role-Based Access Control (RBAC) system that reduced unnecessary privilege exposure across the application.

The knowledge I needed to do this work came from multiple disciplines. My core cybersecurity courses provided the technical grounding in how injection attacks, XSS payloads, and CSRF tokens work at a code level. My ethics and philosophy coursework taught me how to weigh decisions when remediating vulnerabilities in a live environment, where a poorly executed fix can introduce new risks and where communication with stakeholders matters as much as the technical solution. My IDS 300W course sharpened my ability to document findings clearly and present vulnerability assessments in a way that non-technical stakeholders could understand.

After my internship, I extended this skill through a SQL Injection Testing Lab using DVWA and Mutillidae as intentionally vulnerable environments with SQLMap as the testing tool. This reinforced my understanding of attack vectors from an offensive perspective, which is important for anyone doing defensive security work. Job ads for cybersecurity analyst and application security roles consistently list OWASP Top 10 knowledge and vulnerability remediation experience as requirements. This skill directly addresses that demand.

Cloud Computing with AWS

Cloud infrastructure has become the default operating environment for most organizations, and AWS remains the dominant platform. I earned my AWS Certified Cloud Practitioner certification in March 2025, validating my working knowledge of core services including EC2, S3, IAM, VPC, and CloudFormation. The certification process pushed me to develop both breadth and depth, understanding not just what each service does but how they interact in a secure architecture.

The most directly applicable skill within cloud computing is Identity and Access Management. AWS IAM covers managing users, groups, roles, and least-privilege policies, which maps closely to what enterprise IAM tools like Active Directory and Okta do at scale. My coursework in network security and systems administration gave me the technical foundation to understand IAM policy logic, while my Risk Management and Insurance minor gave me a framework for thinking about access control as a risk mitigation strategy. Treating unnecessary permissions as a quantifiable risk changes how you approach IAM design.

This interdisciplinary connection is something I learned to recognize through courses like IDS 300W and my risk management coursework, where technical decisions carry risk and business implications. Entry-level cloud security and IAM analyst job postings at firms like Booz Allen Hamilton, Leidos, and CACI regularly list AWS experience and cloud security fundamentals as key requirements. This skill directly addresses that gap.

Security Operations and Threat Detection

Security operations represents the daily work of most cybersecurity analysts, including monitoring systems, detecting anomalies, investigating alerts, and responding to incidents. I developed this skill through coursework and lab work using Splunk for SIEM log analysis, Wireshark for packet capture and network traffic analysis, and Nmap for host discovery and port scanning. Each tool serves a different phase of the security operations workflow: detection, analysis, and reconnaissance.

Splunk is a tool I have encountered repeatedly in professional job postings as a minimum qualification for SOC analyst roles. Learning to write SPL queries, build dashboards, and correlate events across data sources transfers directly to a working environment. Wireshark reinforced my understanding of TCP/IP at a protocol level, knowledge I first encountered in networking coursework, and applying it in a lab context made those concepts concrete. Nmap gave me an offensive perspective on network exposure, which sharpened my ability to think about what an attacker sees.

My coursework in security operations taught me the structured methodology behind incident response, while my broader academic experience in risk management and interdisciplinary studies taught me how to communicate findings clearly and prioritize action based on impact. In security operations, technical accuracy matters, but so does the ability to explain what an alert means to a team under pressure. Both skills came from different parts of my program, and this portfolio reflects how they came together.

Conclusion

Looking back at my time at Old Dominion University, the most important thing I have learned is that cybersecurity problems do not respect disciplinary boundaries. A SQL injection vulnerability is a technical problem, but understanding why it exists, what the consequences are, and how to communicate it to stakeholders requires thinking that comes from ethics, risk management, and communication disciplines. My interdisciplinary coursework, particularly IDS 300W, trained me to frame problems that way, which made me more effective in every technical course that followed.

I came into this program focused almost entirely on the technical side. I finished understanding that documentation, communication, and risk-based thinking are what separate good cybersecurity analysts from great ones, and that most of those skills came from courses that did not feel directly related to security at the time. That is exactly what interdisciplinary learning is supposed to do.

The three skills in this portfolio represent where I am strongest and most employable. More than that, they represent how different courses, experiences, and disciplines converged into a coherent professional identity. I am graduating in May 2026 prepared not just to pass certifications, but to do the actual work of keeping systems and people safer. This portfolio is the evidence of that.
